Teaching Machines to Listen

The Problem

The Cracks in a Powerful Machine

Invoca's AI Studio was the company's most ambitious product bet. It let customers train custom LLM models on their own call data — building signals that could automatically detect whether a caller booked an appointment, converted to a sale, expressed frustration, or hit any of dozens of other outcomes specific to their business. The feature launched in early 2025 and it worked. Accuracy was high. The use cases were wide-ranging. Customers who got it working loved it.

But getting it working was the problem.

The v1 workflow dropped users straight into training — reviewing calls one at a time, marking them as true or false examples of whatever they were trying to detect. Each round required evaluating 20 true calls and 20 false calls to help the model learn. It was meticulous, manual, and demanding. And it assumed users already knew exactly what they wanted to find.

Many of them thought they knew — but their understanding shifted as they started reviewing calls. They'd encounter edge cases they hadn't anticipated, call types that blurred the lines of their original definition, nuances in their own data that only became visible through the act of looking. The workflow was simultaneously a training tool and an exploration tool, and it was only designed to be the first.

"After round zero, you're sort of locked in to what you've fed it."
Radhika Sharma — Analytics Manager, Invoca
Research

Eight Hours of Someone Else's Time

I worked with PM Zach Cohen to scope a research effort — we interviewed four Invoca team members who spent their days guiding customers through AI signal creation. These were the people who lived inside the workflow's gaps every day: building signals on behalf of customers, interpreting ambiguous definitions, and coaching people through a tool that was supposed to be self-service.

What made these interviews unusually valuable was that these team members weren't just observers of the problem — they were power users of the product themselves. They were in the workflow daily, training signals with their own hands, hitting the same friction points customers hit. The difference was they had the expertise to work around those friction points. Customers didn't.

The pattern was immediate and unanimous. Invoca had quietly built an entire human infrastructure around the workflow's weaknesses.

Every new AI Gold customer now received 8 hours of dedicated time from the analytics services team — a policy implemented after six months of watching customers struggle. For larger enterprise accounts like T-Mobile, the arrangement was even more involved: contracted hours on a yearly basis with a dedicated analyst handling all signal creation.

"The process is pretty manual. It's not as direct as I would like it to be, or not as straightforward as I would like it to be."
Sofia Contreras — Senior Analytics Services Manager, Invoca

The team wasn't just training signals. They were doing the thinking that the product should have been doing — anticipating edge cases, pre-classifying call types, and building definitions through back-and-forth conversations with customers before ever touching the workflow. Sofia described her process for telco clients: before round zero, she'd map out every possible call scenario and present each one to the customer. Only then would she start training. The product offered none of this guidance. It just showed you a call and asked: true or false?

Mitch Williams estimated that maybe 50% or fewer customers were even going through the standard signal definition form. The rest were getting hands-on help through informal channels — Slack messages to CSMs, ad hoc calls with analytics team members, whatever it took.

The product worked. But it only worked with a person standing next to it, explaining what to do. That doesn't scale.

"What we're finding is that customers need help and assistance as they define signals. What's the best way to define a signal?"
Mitch Williams — Senior Business Value Lead, Invoca
Problem Framing

The Bookends Problem

Our research crystallized two distinct failure points — and they were at opposite ends of the workflow.

The front door was missing. What made a good use case for an AI signal? What didn't? The workflow had no answer. It assumed users arrived with a clear definition and just needed a training mechanism. In reality, customers often didn't know what their calls contained until they started looking. One example made this concrete: a customer called Lumos started training a signal for new business sales opportunities without guidance on how to scope it or awareness that the signal had low prevalence in their call data. They trained for thirteen rounds and still couldn't get acceptable accuracy. When Sofia came in, she did a quick transcript search, properly scoped the signal, and reached 90–95% predicted accuracy by round four.

The difference wasn't the technology. It was knowing what to aim at before you started training.

The back door was duct-taped shut. Once users launched a signal, the product gave them an "estimated accuracy" score — but it couldn't tell them how the signal actually performed in the wild. Real-world accuracy could swing 5–10% from the training prediction, and the product had no mechanism for ongoing validation. Analytics team members were running accuracy tests by hand — pulling random call samples, listening to them, comparing the signal's prediction to reality, and recording results in spreadsheets.

The workflow's middle — the actual model training — was strong. But without a guided entry point and without automated validation, the whole system depended on human scaffolding at both ends.

"We find that the predicted accuracy in the UI is not necessarily the best way of measuring your signal's success."
Sofia Contreras — Senior Analytics Services Manager, Invoca
Research Insight

They Don't Want to Dig. They Want to Know.

Mitch Williams framed it most directly. In the current state, you had to build the signals first, then run analysis once they were in place. The opportunity was to flip that entirely — surface insights first, then use those insights to inform what signals to build.

"Flipping that on its head — providing insights first that inform what signals to build — I think would be kind of cool."
Mitch Williams — Senior Business Value Lead, Invoca

Brian Coulombe had a different angle on the same frustration. He kept returning to a wish: that the system could simply understand his definition — what he was trying to accomplish — and work backward from there, rather than making him hunt through calls to prove it.

"I wish I could just tell it my definition... I wish it could see my definition to just get it."
Brian Coulombe — Analytics Services, Invoca

Brian, Sofia, Radhika, and Mitch were logging into the same interface, training the same models, and wrestling with the same limitations as external customers — often on behalf of those customers. Their frustrations carried the weight of hundreds of hours of direct experience. Radhika reinforced that the front end of the workflow was where the most foundational friction lived.

"That is a pretty big friction point at a very foundational part of the entire Studio AI setup process."
Radhika Sharma — Analytics Manager, Invoca
The Concept

A New Front Door

I designed the long-term vision in collaboration with Zach. We targeted both bookend problems, phased over time. The concept reimagined the workflow in four stages.

Step 1
Topic Explorer
Surface what's in the data before asking users to train anything
Step 2
Signal Blueprint
Define the signal with structured guidance, edge cases, and candidacy checks
Step 3
LLM-as-a-Judge
Two AIs train the model — human arbitrates only where they disagree
Step 4
Validation
Automated accuracy testing on live call data with honest feedback states
Step 1
Topic Explorer
Instead of dropping users into call review, we surfaced topics automatically from their call data — cards showing themes the system had already identified, with references to specific call snippets as evidence. We were targeting two buckets: anticipated use cases that every customer in a given vertical would likely need, and unknown unknowns — anomalies and patterns the customer wouldn't have thought to look for, pulled automatically from their data. Don't make me hunt. Show me what's there.
Step 2
Signal Blueprint
Once a user selected a topic, this step helped them define what they actually wanted the signal to find. What should it include? What should it exclude? The page provided structured guidance — and critically, it recommended some use cases as good candidates for AI signals and flagged others that weren't. AI signals were weaker at certain things — empathy and emotion-based detection, for example — and the v1 workflow never told users that. They'd train for weeks and discover the limitation the hard way. This step moved Sofia's entire pre-round-zero process into the product.
Step 3
LLM-as-a-Judge Training
The existing manual training stayed as an option, but the concept introduced a new path: a second LLM would evaluate calls alongside the custom model. Where the two AIs agreed, no human input was needed. Where they disagreed, the user became the tiebreaker. The user was still in the loop — but only at decision points that actually required human judgment. They weren't reviewing forty calls per round anymore. They were arbitrating the cases that mattered.

This was the design problem that made this project genuinely hard from a UX perspective. LLM outputs are probabilistic, not deterministic. The subject matter experts evaluating them aren't engineers — they're marketing analysts and QA managers. Evaluation criteria are inherently subjective. And the feedback loop between what you train and what you get is opaque. Designing an interface that makes all of this legible to non-technical users without oversimplifying the underlying complexity — that's the core challenge of AI product design.

Step 4
Automated Validation
The goal was to productize what the analytics team had been doing manually with spreadsheets: automated accuracy testing on live call data, with feedback mechanisms for different outcomes. The validation step needed to be honest — a product that only tells you the good news isn't building trust. We were designing the feedback to acknowledge limitations directly, including recommending that some use cases switch to keyword-based signals or other detection methods when AI wasn't the right tool.

Design Challenge

Designing for Machines That Guess

Most product design operates on a simple contract: the user does a thing, the system gives a deterministic result. Click a button, get a confirmation. Enter a search query, get matching results. The logic is mechanical and the output is predictable.

AI model training breaks that contract in every direction at once. The outputs are probabilistic. The people evaluating them aren't engineers — they're marketing analysts and QA managers. The evaluation criteria are subjective. And the feedback loop between training and results is opaque — measured in hours, not milliseconds. Every design decision in this concept was shaped by that reality.

There's no design system for this problem. No established pattern library. The frameworks have to be invented for each new context — and the one we built for Invoca's signal training workflow was a genuine contribution to how this problem can be approached.

Vision

Where It Was Headed

I was designing the next iteration when the project ended — I left Invoca before we could move from concept into build. Here's where the vision was going.

Industry-aware signal templates. The Topic Explorer could surface industry-specific recommendations informed by patterns across Invoca's entire customer base — a form of aggregated intelligence that would make each new customer's setup faster than the last. Healthcare customers need appointment scheduling signals. Telco needs product-line detection. Financial services needs compliance flags. Rather than every customer starting from zero.

Continuous validation loops. The concept addressed validation at signal launch, but the longer-term vision was ongoing accuracy monitoring. Signals drift as call patterns change — a promotion ends, a new product launches, agent scripts get updated. The system needed to detect accuracy degradation before customers noticed it, and surface re-training recommendations proactively.

Progressive automation of the human loop. The LLM-as-a-Judge model was designed to reduce human effort, not eliminate it. But as the system accumulated more training data across customers and verticals, the ratio could shift further. Signals with high confidence from both models could skip human review entirely. The goal was a system that got smarter about when it needed a person — and stopped asking when it didn't.

Reflection

The Work That Almost Shipped

This is the hardest kind of project to put in a portfolio — the one that was right but wasn't finished. The concept validated cleanly. The research was clear. The stakeholders were aligned. The interactive prototype told the story. And then the project ended before it could become a product.

But the work mattered beyond what shipped. The research exposed a structural problem — that the product's self-service promise was being subsidized by an invisible human layer — and the concept addressed it at the system level, not with patches. That's staff-level design work: seeing past the individual screens to the system dynamics that determine whether a product can actually scale.

If I were picking this up tomorrow, I'd push harder on two things. First, the validation step needed more user research with external customers. Our internal team members were genuine users of the product — but they also bring expertise that masks usability problems a less experienced user would surface. The concept needs to work for the customer who's training their first signal without Sofia or Radhika guiding them through it. Second, the LLM-as-a-Judge interface and the validation feedback system were both still in active iteration — those are the moments where the design either builds trust or erodes it, and getting the information hierarchy right there is critical.
